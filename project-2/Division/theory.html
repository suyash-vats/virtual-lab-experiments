Division Operations

  • Nikhilam Method

    • The Nikhilam Sutra translates to "All from 9 and the last from 10." This approach involves subtracting the digits of the divisor from the nearest base and then using the result to perform the division. This technique simplifies the division process, especially when the divisor is slightly less or slightly more than a power of 10.
    Example:-
    Let's divide 43 by 9 using the Nikhilam method.
    Identify the Base: The nearest base for 9 is 10.
    Find the Nikhilam Complement: The complement of 9 is 1 (10 - 9 = 1).
    Divide the Dividend:
    Split 43 into two parts: 4 and 3.
    Quotient Calculation: Divide 4 (the left part) by 9. Since 4 is less than 9, the quotient is 0.
    Remainder Adjustment: Multiply the quotient (0) by the complement (1) and subtract it from 3 (the right part).
    The remainder is 3.
    Final Answer: The quotient is 4, and the remainder is 7.
    So, 43 ÷ 9 = 4 remainder 7.

  • Paravartya Yojayet Method

    • This method involves transposing the divisor and applying it to simplify the division. Instead of traditional long division, the Paravartya Yojayet method transforms the division process by using complements and adjustments, making it easier and often quicker.
    Example:-
    Let's divide 256 by 12 using the Paravartya Yojayet method.
    Transpose the Divisor:
    The divisor is 12. The first digit (1) remains the same, and the second digit (2) is transposed to become -2.
    Divide the Dividend:
    Write the dividend, 256.
    First Step: The first digit of the dividend (2) is divided by the first digit of the divisor (1), giving a quotient of 2.
    Second Step: Multiply the quotient by the transposed part of the divisor (-2). This gives 2 × -2 = -4.
    Third Step: Subtract this result (-4) from the next digit of the dividend (5). So, 5 - (-4) = 9.
    Final Step: Combine the quotient from the first digit and the remainder (which becomes the new number to divide) to get the final quotient and remainder.
    Result:
    After performing the steps, the quotient is 21, and the remainder is 4.
    So, 256 ÷ 12 = 21 remainder 4.

  • Anurupyena Method

    • The Anurupyena method involves choosing a convenient base or factor related to the divisor, making the division process more straightforward. This approach is particularly useful when the divisor is a number that doesn’t align easily with the standard bases like 10 or 100.
    Example:-
    Let’s divide 84 by 7 using the Anurupyena method.
    Choose a Suitable Base:
    The divisor is 7. A convenient base close to 7 is 10. We can use a factor of 10/7 (approximately 1.428) to adjust the numbers.
    Adjust the Dividend and Divisor:
    Multiply both the dividend (84) and the divisor (7) by 10, making the calculation easier:
    Adjusted Dividend:
    84×10=840
    Adjusted Divisor:
    7×10=70
    Now, divide 840 by 70.
    Perform the Division:
    840÷70=12
    Reverse the Adjustment:
    Since we used a factor of 10/7, the quotient remains the same. There’s no need to reverse the adjustment, as the result is accurate.
    So, 84 ÷ 7 = 12.
